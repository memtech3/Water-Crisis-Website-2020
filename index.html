Heavy Metal Water Crisies during the Turn of the Century

In the late 19th and early 20th century, there was an “epidemic” (Clay & Troesken, n.d.) of lead poisoning due to usage of lead plumbing combined with acidic water, causing harmful amounts of lead to leach into the water system. One specific instance of early 20th century lead poisoning was in Lowell, Michigan. In 1900, the average lead level in Lowell tap water was 169 times the current EPA guidelines for lead contamination (Troesken, n.d., 10). Although it is believed that the majority of the lead content came from lead service lines, industrial pollution of water sources almost certainly contributed to the unusually high levels of lead contamination (Troesken, n.d., 8). One of the reasons that the lead service lines leeched such high amounts of lead, compared to other lead based systems, was that the water source that Lowell had recently switched to was very acidic (Clay & Troesken, n.d.), causing the lead to be more soluble. Additionally, the lead pipes were relatively new, meaning that they lacked a protective layer of corrosion to prevent water from coming into direct contact with the lead (Troesken, n.d., 2). The health effects of the high lead levels in Lowell were severe and long lasting, often accompanied by poor diagnosis and treatment and, for those who survived, chronic physical and mental health issues.


Flint Michigan Heavy Metal Water Crisis

Due to the popularity and longevity of lead plumbing, there have been several notable (and many more less publicized) instances of lead contamination in the 21st century. Of today’s water crisis, the Flint Water Crisis is probably the most popular and best documented, due to its popularity. The Flint Water Crisis was a 4 year long water crisis, during which primarily lead, but also other contaminants such as legionnaires disease, were prevalent in the Flint Water System due to poor management and not following industry standard water safety practices. The direct cause of the Flint Water Crisis was almost certainly poverty due to the GM downsizings in the 1980’s (click on the graph to the left), leading to a slow and painful economic decline in Flint, ending with the state of Michigan taking over Flint’s finances and making the decision to switch water sources. Had Flint been a prosperous city, the lead service pipes would have either been replaced or properly maintained and the city of Flint wouldn’t have been placed into the hands of a state appointed emergency manager, who lead the city so poorly. To conclude, the Flint Water Crisis was almost entirely due to poverty, as without poverty, Flint would not have had to switch its water source from the Detroit Water System to the Flint River.
